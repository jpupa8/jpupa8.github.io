Baseball's 2021 crackdown on Pine Tar, an analysis.

Using Data Science to determine the effectiveness and rationale of Major League Baseball's midseason increase in enforcement against the use of grip enhancing substances by pitchers.

Joseph Pupa, May 16, 2022.

Introduction: Two things have long been truths in Major League Baseball: First, its a numbers game, average, on-base-percentage, spin-rate, launch-angle, wins-above-replacement. Over the years, the game has evolved to track the performance of players is every way imaginable, with todays revolution being in the use of radar and other technology systems to track everything down to the acceleration of the bat head on a swing. The other truth is the game's long history of cheating. Virtually everyone is familiar with the Black Sox scanadal of nearly a century ago, or the steroid-era led by the likes of Barry Bonds, Mark McGuire, Samy Sosa, and Alex Rodriguez. In recent years, the Houston Astros have been taken to task for their use of electronics to steal signs en route to a World Series victory. On June 20 of 2021, however, the commisioners office cracked down on a different kind of cheating, the use of pine tar or "sticky stuff" (pine tar and other substances will be refered to as such for the remainder of this document) as it is refered to by pitchers around the sport. Allegedly, pitchers used pine tar to gain extra grip on the ball, thus allowing their spin rate and velocity to increase, making their pitches harder to hit, thus slowing down offenses around the game. In this tutorial, I seek to answer three primary questions. First, as it relates to the pitchers, did this crackdown have an effect on the the features of pitching they felt this exploit gave them a leg up in? Second, did this crack down have an impact on offense around the sport, ie did hitters benefit from what the league saw as leveling the playing field. Third, is there an identifiable linear relationship between the changes they desired to see in both pitching and offense. Answering these questions will allow us to more definitively see whether the use of "sticky stuff" was appropriately combated and to judge the success of their actions against it.

Credit: All pitching data for this project acquired through Baseball Savant, a MLB Advanced Media service specializing in statistical tracking and search. (baseballsavant.mlb.com). All batting data for this project was acquired through FanGraphs, a popular baseball statistics and sabermetrics tracking and reporting outlet. (Fangraphs.com)

Data Collection: For purposes of this project, I will be using pandas, numpy, and matplot lib to help organize and wrangle the data needed to answer the questions posed above. First, it is important to define the data that I will import below. The sticky stuff crackdown occured on June 20, 2021. In order to quantify the impacts of the crackdown, and the use of sticky stuff itself, I have collected two data sets from Baseball Savant (credited above). First, a set called before, which is comprised of all pitching information from opening day of the 2021 season through the date of the crackdown (totaling 80 days of the season). Second, a set called after, is comprised of the following 85 days after the crackdown was announced. I have chosen to extend the second data set by 5 days in order to account for the five day all star break that occurs in mid July, in an effort to roughly balance the amount of baseball played in each data set. Importing this data and printing can be seen in the code below.

Examining Pitching after the Crackdown:

In order to see what impact, if any, the use of sticky stuff and subsequent crackdown had on pitching. We need to examine what several key stats for pitchers looked like before and after the change. The stats most impacted by sticky stuff according to hitters, pundits, and others around the sport were (Definition provided by MLB StatCast and Baseball Savant) Spin Rate, a measurement of the baseball in revolutions per minute after leaving the pitchers hand, Pitch Velocity, which is self explanatory, and whiffs, which is a measure of swinging strikes recorded by a pitcher. From the hitters perspective, at first look we will look at batting average, a very simple statistic of hits divided by total at bats, as well as launch speed, which represents the average speed the ball comes off the bat, an important denoter to whether hitters are seeing and timing pitchers at a quality mark. In the following code, data from each pitcher is sorted in various lists in order to then create graphs of these stats off of each pitcher using previously credited python libraries (matplotlib). Since we want solid sample sizes for pitch counts, I have chosen to omit any pitcher that did not throw at least 500 pitches. On average in Major League Baseball, an inning is about 15 pitches. This means that our 500 pitch threshold equates to about 33 innings of work needed throughout the first 2.5 months of the season in order to qualify for these plots and statistics. This is an effort to not include pitchers that appeared once for one or two terrible innings or only appeared once and then was sent back down to the minor leagues. By installing a pitch threshold, we will only see mainstay, legitimate Major League Pitchers (those who matter for statistical analaysis like this).

Analysis of Pitching statistics before and after Crackdown:

Of the five key statistics, four show the desired impact Major League Baseball had when it instituted the increased enforcement on grip enchacing substances. After the crackdown, the batting average against all pitchers (on average) rose by nearly 10 points. Average pitch velocity saw a decline by .1 MPH. Spin Rate saw a decline of 66 RPM. Finally, average Whiffs induced saw a decline of 7. Interestingly, Launch Speed decreased after the crackdown by about .6 MPH, I would tend to attribute this mostly to the average pitch coming in slower, thus, when hit, will also leave the bat somewhat slower. Beside Launch Speed, the crackdown on pine tar and other substances seems to have worked from the pitching point of view. However, did this have an impact on more specfic offensive production categories? After all, these changes were implemented as a result of what the league saw as pitching over powering hitting. We have already seen an uptick in batting average after the crackdown, but what about other key offensive stats. The code below will show the difference before and after the crackdown in several other offensive stats such at Walk Rate (BB%), Strike Out Rate (K%), and On Base Percentage by first importing batting stats acquired through FanGraphs, before applying a very similar process as we completed above to chart the differences.

As seen in the graphs above, all of the offensive stats we observed improved after the crackdown on pine tar and other like substances used by pitchers. On Base Percentage climbed by nearly 10 points. Walk rates increased and Strikeout rates decreased, all signs that hitters (and more generally, offenses) were more prolific after the crackdown.

We have definitively shown that the crackdown on pine tar resulted in two things:

1) Statistics usually associated with talented pitching (High Spin Rate, High Whiffs, High Velocity, Low AVG Against) all moved against pitchers
2) Statistics usually associated with high quality batting (Low K Rate, High BB Rate, and High On Base Percentage) all moved in favor of batters.

At first thought, it would seem that the crackdown had its desired impact. However, it is vital to identify whether there exists a relationship between the increases and decreases in respective hitting and pitching statistics. In other words, can we show definitively that the changes seen after the crackdown are the result of the rule changes (ie not a coincidence)? Using Linear Regression and Fitting, we will determine how much of a relationship exists between these stats, in order to conclude whether the change in pitching stats (as a result of the rule enforcement) is the reason for the uptick in offensive production. As a specific example, We have seen that on base percentage rose after the ban, but is there a true linear relationship between a decline in various pitching statistics and a rise in on base percentage. Below is code that will carry out these hypothesis' tests and make these determinations. Specifically, this code will compile monthly averages of the pitching and batting stats we have been discussing. Then, using the statsmodels library, we will conduct several OLS/Least Squares analysis tests to identify what correlations/linear relationships exist, this first requires a bit of data wrangling/organizing, as NaN values will need to be removed to ensure proper calculations, and then the actual tests will be created and ran.

For each of these tests, we will primarily be focused on the resulting R^2 value. R^2 is represented as a value between 0 and 1 and provides a numerical indicator as to how much of a linear relationship/correlation exists between two (or more) variables. Intuitively, an R^2 of 1 indicates a perfect correlation, and 0 represents no relationship. As we stated earlier, Identifying relationships between the various statistics we have been anaylzing is the final key needed to conclusively determine the success of the crackdown and whether its rationale was correct.

Conclusions:

In the introduction of this project, we sought to identify several things related to Major League Baseball's crackdown on the use of pinetar and other like substances in June of the 2021 baseball regular season.

First, we sought to confirm that several key pitching statistics had seen a decrease after the crackdown as compared to before, and, according to the charts and printed averages during the "before" and "after" periods, we confirmed just that.

Second, we sought to confirm a similar trend with respect to hitters, specifically, whether there was an increase in key offensive statistics after the crackdown as opposed to before, and, once again, according to the charts and printed averages, this was indentified as occuring.

Third, and most importantly, after seeing that both pitchers and batters were impacted by the changes, it was vital to identify if these various batting and pitching statistics shared linear relationships with eachother. In other words, to confirm or deny the effectiveness and necesity of the crackdown on substance usage by contextualizing the the various pitching and batting statistics. In doing so, we saw that there are strong relationships between pitch spin rate and strike outs, pitch velocity and on base percentage, and, strikeouts and on base percentage. We also indentified a moderately significant relationship between pitch spin rate and walk rate.

Given the liner relationships identified, along with the identified changes in various statistics before and after the increased enforcement, we can conclude that Major League Baseball's crackdown on pinetar usage was not only successful, but can be definitively pointed to as the catalyst for the changes seen in the game after the crackdown. The commisioners office sought to help out offense and slightly restrict pitching, and as we saw in the first two parts of this project, they were successful in this. Second, their logic with respect to what characteristics of the game needed to be influenced in order to create the outcomes we just described as successful, was sound. The league desired more men on base, and more runs, and while lower spinrate and velocity did not directly have a correlation with higher on base percentage, they did correlate with higher walk rates and lower strikeout rates, which DO correlate with higher on base percentage. Thus banning grip enhancing substances inflenced the correct attributes in pitchers that would cause the desires changes in batting. The league understood what needed to be changed in order to create a better sense of balance between pitching and hitting, and understood what was creating the uneven playing field, and as a result, their increased enforcement against the use of grip enchancing substances resulted in effectively taking advantage of the relationship between various statistical aspects of both pitching and hitting, in order to produce the exact results they were seeking.
